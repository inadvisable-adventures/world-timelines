## Items

- In a test run (2026-07-03) collecting 100 entries from the Wikipedia multistream dump, the rejection rate was ~93% (1,411 rejected out of 1,511 considered). The main causes are missing location data and missing/unparseable dates. This is worth investigating: can we recover more entries by (a) falling back to other fields within the same infobox (e.g. a country or region field as a coarse location), (b) following wiki links to related articles that do have coordinates, or (c) supplementing with external datasets such as Wikidata, GeoNames, or the World Historical Gazetteer? Each cause of rejection should probably be tracked separately in the status log to make the tradeoffs clearer.

- `recurring_event` is a Wikipedia infobox with 4K+ instances. These seem like they would be an interesting addition to the timeline.

- The data model now supports rich map representations (polygon-with-holes, multipolygon, time-correlated paths, uncertain/disputed locations), but Wikipedia infoboxes almost certainly do not supply enough data to populate these automatically. Come back to this and brainstorm: What infobox fields or categories could feed multipolygon boundaries (e.g. does Wikidata have historical boundary geometries)? What other data sources could provide political boundary polygons over time, trade route paths, or disputed-location metadata? Are there structured datasets (e.g. NHGIS, the World Historical Gazetteer, Pleiades, the Silk Road GIS project) that could be ingested alongside Wikipedia?

- The `kibbutz` infobox currently falls into the `other` category, but kibbutzim are simultaneously cooperative communities and economic enterprises. This raises a broader question: should `business` and `pol_mil_organization` be merged into a single `organization` category? Both cover entities that are collective, institutional, and often geographically bounded — the distinction between "political/military" and "commercial" blurs for things like the East India Company, a kibbutz, a medieval guild, or a city-state. Merging them would simplify categorization and reduce edge-case judgment calls, at the cost of some granularity.

- A Wikipedia article about a person is not simply a record of a person — it is often a chronicle organized around that person's life. A notable murder is documented through the article about the victim. A political figure's article is structured around elections won, offices held, exile periods, and rehabilitation. A scientist's article traces degree programs, key discoveries, controversies, and institutional affiliations. When we ingest such an article as a single entry with a start date and an end date, we collapse all of that structure into a flat blob. This raises a question that cuts across the data model, the ingestion pipeline, and the UI at once: should a Wikipedia person article produce one entry (the lifespan) or many (each meaningful phase or event within it)? And if we split, what is the right unit — a named life phase, an officeholder incumbency, a specific documented incident? There is no clean answer, but the current approach already creates artifacts: a murder victim appears in the `person` category with a death date, with no indication that the historically significant moment is the killing rather than the life. Any move toward sub-entries would need to handle who defines the phases, how they relate to the existing `event` category, whether they share the parent person's location data, and how the UI surfaces the relationship between a person and the events that constitute their record.

- Coordinate recall is capped at ~7.4% by pure-wikitext extraction (investigated 2026-07-06). A sample of `no-coords-found` entries showed the current extractor already captures nearly all wikitext-embedded coordinates; the remaining misses are dominated by cases that carry NO machine-readable coordinates in the article text: Wikidata-backed infoboxes with an empty `|coordinates=` field (or `{{… wikidata|coordinates}}`), `{{coord missing|Country}}` maintenance tags, empty template skeletons (`|latd=`, `|latitude=` blank), `{{Geographic Location}}` navigation compasses (no coords), and — for persons/events — only place *names*. Locator-map SVGs (e.g. "X in Greater London.svg") are static thematic vector maps with no embedded geodata (confirmed via Commons). Meaningfully higher recall requires resolving coordinates from outside the article text: (a) the Wikidata dump (P625 coordinate-location, keyed by the article's wikibase item), or (b) geocoding place names / following wikilinks to place articles that do have coordinates, via a gazetteer (GeoNames, World Historical Gazetteer). Overlaps with the first parking-lot item; this is the concrete next step for coordinate coverage.

  What P625 is: Wikidata identifies items by Q-numbers (e.g. Paris = `Q90`) and properties by P-numbers (e.g. `P31` "instance of", `P569` "date of birth"). **`P625` is the "coordinate location" property** — it holds an item's latitude/longitude (Paris `Q90` → `P625` = 48°51′24″N, 2°21′8″E; see https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Property:P625). This is exactly why so many of our `no-coords-found` entries are still mappable: the entity usually *does* have a `P625` value in Wikidata, it's just not written into the article wikitext (the infobox pulls it from Wikidata at render time). Enrichment = resolve each article to its Q-number, then read `P625`.

  Access options for Wikidata P625, and their scale tradeoffs:
  - **Wikidata MCP server** (https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Wikidata:MCP) — NOT connected to the current Claude Code session (checked 2026-07-06; only Gmail/Calendar/Drive MCPs are wired up). To enable it: `claude mcp add --transport http wikidata <url-from-the-MCP-page>` (then restart the session so the tools load), or add it to `.mcp.json`. Best suited to interactive/agent-style lookups, NOT bulk enrichment — a per-entity round-trip for ~797k entities would be far too slow.
  - **SPARQL over HTTPS** (`query.wikidata.org/sparql`) — reachable now via `WebFetch` with no new setup. Good for targeted/spot-check batches (a few thousand at most, subject to rate limits and query timeouts); not viable for the full 797k no-coords set.
  - **Wikidata dump** (truthy dump, or a coordinate-only extract) — the proper batch approach: join P625 to our entries by article title → Q-id. Tens of GB and effectively another ingester pass, but the only option that scales to the whole corpus. This is the heavier feature to plan.

  Suggested sequencing: first prototype the enrichment on a small batch via SPARQL/`WebFetch` (option a in the discussion — proof of concept, no new setup) to confirm the P625 join actually lifts the hit rate, THEN decide whether to commit to the dump-based batch pipeline. If the Wikidata MCP is connected in future, use it for interactive validation/spot-checks, not bulk enrichment.

- More lanesets for the timeline: lanesets based on the *geographical understanding of a given era* — "how the world saw itself" at a point in time — rather than modern geography. E.g. a laneset reflecting the known/mapped world at the BCE/CE changeover (the Roman/Hellenistic oikoumene, the Han view, the Indian subcontinent, terra incognita beyond), or other snapshots (a medieval T-O map division, the Age of Exploration world, etc.). Each such laneset would divide the surface by a historical worldview and note its criterion + date in the description. Ties into the era→lane machinery; shape data would come from historical cartography rather than Natural Earth.

- The `officeholder` infobox (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Template:Infobox_officeholder) raises an interesting distinction between an organizational office and the person who holds it. The office itself is a continuous institution with potentially non-contiguous timeline ranges (e.g. gaps during abolition or interregnum). Individual holders are semantically linked to the office, and complications like schisms mean incumbencies can overlap (e.g. antipopes). This layered structure — office as entity, holder as person, incumbency as a relationship with its own time range — adds meaningful depth to historical understanding and is worth modeling explicitly.
